Final Self-Assessment · 2025/2026

It’s been a valuable period of growth in all areas of English. By working on a wide range of tasks, I was able to identify my strengths, reflect on challenges, and understand better how I learn. Below is my self-assessment divided into the four main language skills, along with a comparison of where I started and where I am now:

Reading

Start: B2
Now: C1

Listening

Start: B1+
Now: C1

Speaking

Start: B1
Now: C1

Writing

Start: B2
Now: C1

Reading

Reading remains one of my stronger skills, partly because I have enjoyed literature my whole life. This year I deliberately chose more demanding texts: Virginia Woolf's Orlando and H. G. Wells's The Time Machine stretched me with literary and late-Victorian English, while a terminology-heavy reading response for Educational Communication pushed me through dense academic vocabulary such as proxemics and kinesics. Woolf's half-page sentences and shifting narrative voice were the hardest part - I often had to reread a passage before it settled. I feel more comfortable now with sustained literary prose, and I want to keep building literary and period vocabulary by annotating as I read rather than guessing from context.

Listening

Listening is the skill I evidenced least formally this year - I did not produce a dedicated listening artefact - but I kept practising informally through podcasts, news, and British series and films. I can follow various accents, especially British, and fast speech more easily than before, though I still struggle with informal slang and muffled audio. My clear priority for next term is to turn this informal listening into proper artefacts - for example, notes on a podcast or a listening task I design - so the skill is documented, not only practised.

Speaking

Speaking is still the area where I feel least secure, and this year gave me concrete evidence of why. I recorded a video presentation on communication rules for teachers and students, and both my teacher and a peer reviewer responded well to the interactivity - the guiding questions and the closing challenge - but they pointed out that my delivery was mostly monotone, with too little variation in tone and pace. This is the same weakness I noticed last year in my intonation and dramatic-reading work, so it is clearly recurring rather than a one-off. Going forward I want to mark stress and pauses directly in my scripts and rehearse with deliberate pitch variation, especially for classroom delivery.

Writing

Writing was again my clearest area of growth. This year I wrote a reflective essay on Rosenberg's Life-Enriching Education and, most demandingly, an empirical research paper in full APA format - abstract, literature review, statistics and references. The research paper was the first time I sustained a genuinely academic register across a long text, hedging claims precisely and integrating sources without overquoting; it showed me I can move beyond the conversational voice I tend to default to in reflective writing. I still produce some over-long sentences and want to express ideas more economically and to raise my register deliberately rather than by accident.

Future Goals

  • Pass a formal English exam at C1 level (e.g. CAE)
  • Work deliberately on intonation and pacing to overcome monotone delivery
  • Produce proper listening artefacts next term so the skill is documented, not only practised
  • Keep practising a formal academic register alongside reflective writing
  • Facilitate at least one learning activity entirely in English
